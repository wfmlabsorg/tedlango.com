Yirgacheffe was the coffee that made me realize I'd been drinking the wrong thing for years. It's a small district in southern Ethiopia, sitting up around 1,700–2,200 meters, and at that altitude the cherries ripen slowly enough to pack in sugars and acids that lower-grown coffee just doesn't get.

The thing to understand about Ethiopian coffee is that nobody's really planting "varietals" the way they do in Colombia or Panama. What grows here is a sprawling mix of local heirloom landraces — thousands of them, mostly undocumented, descended from the wild *Coffea arabica* that originated in these forests. That genetic chaos is *why* the cup is so wild. You don't get one clean note; you get a bouquet.

**What to look for.** Two processing styles dominate, and they taste like two different coffees:

- **Washed** — the classic Yirgacheffe. Fruit stripped off before drying, so what's left is transparent and bright: jasmine, bergamot, lemon, a tea-like body that almost disappears. This is the floral, delicate one.
- **Natural** — dried whole in the cherry, which pushes it toward blueberry, strawberry, a heavier sweetness and more body. Louder, jammier.

If you've only had grocery-store "Ethiopian" and found it muddy, it was almost certainly a dark roast that incinerated everything I just described. Yirgacheffe wants a **light roast** and a brew method that gets out of its way — a clean V60 over anything with a lot of body.

**The "why," briefly.** The florals come largely from the heirloom genetics; the brightness comes from altitude (slow ripening → higher acidity); the cleanliness comes from the washed process removing fermentable fruit before it can muddy the cup. Change any one of those three and you get a different coffee. That's the whole appeal of learning origins — the cup is legible if you know what went into it.
