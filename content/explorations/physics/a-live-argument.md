Everything so far in this series has been, more or less, *settled* — old results, firm constraints, a door that closes a little more with each notebook. This one is different. I want to show you something still in motion: a real disagreement between real physicists, in print, right now, with no agreed answer. Because how science argues when it *doesn't* know is, to me, more revealing than any tidy verdict.

The fight is about whether you need [exotic matter](/explorations/physics/negative-energy-is-real/) for a warp drive at all.

## The claim

In 2021, a physicist named Erik Lentz published a paper in *Classical and Quantum Gravity* with a striking result. He had found, he argued, a class of warp "solitons" — self-contained warped-space bubbles — that could move faster than light and be sourced by **purely positive energy.** No exotic matter. No negative energy. Just a configuration of ordinary plasma and electromagnetic fields, the kind of stuff that actually exists.

If that's right, it's enormous. The entire obstacle this series keeps circling — *you need negative energy and you can't get enough* — would simply evaporate for Lentz's design. The press, predictably, lit up. A warp drive with no exotic matter is exactly the headline everyone wants.

I wanted it to be true, which is precisely why I made myself read the rebuttal carefully.

## The rebuttal

In 2022, three respected relativists — Jessica Santiago, Sebastian Schuster, and Matt Visser (the last a leading authority on exactly this kind of question) — published a paper with a blunt title: *Generic warp drives violate the null energy condition.* Their claim: the positive-energy result is an illusion created by looking from only one point of view.

Here's the heart of it, and it's genuinely subtle.

![The crux of the dispute: energy density can look positive to an observer moving with the warp bubble and negative to a differently-moving observer — so 'positive energy' depends on who is measuring.](/img/physics/live-argument.webp)

In relativity, energy density is not an absolute. *Different observers, moving differently through the same patch of space, can measure different energy densities there.* The rule that warp drives must obey — the "no negative energy" condition — requires the energy to come out non-negative for **every** possible observer, not just one.

Santiago, Schuster and Visser argued that Lentz had only checked the energy as measured by observers *riding along with the bubble*. From that one seat, yes, it looks positive. But check the same region from a differently-moving seat, they showed, and the energy goes **negative.** The exotic matter wasn't eliminated — it was hidden behind a particular choice of viewpoint. Generic warp drives, they concluded, violate the condition no matter how you dress them up.

## The defense, and the standoff

Lentz didn't concede. He responded that his *published* version did account for all observers, and that the critics had partly aimed at an earlier draft. The three authors stand by their analysis. And there it sits.

This is the honest part, and I'm not going to tidy it up for you: **the dispute is unresolved.** Both papers are peer-reviewed. Both sets of authors are serious. Lentz himself has acknowledged in print that the disagreement was not settled even after they corresponded directly. There is no referee blowing a final whistle. There's a concrete, checkable question — does the energy stay positive for *every* observer of the exact published metric? — and the two camps have not converged on the answer.

## Two things worth saying plainly

First, even if Lentz turns out to be right about the *sign*, it would not hand anyone a starship. His own design needs an amount of energy comparable to the mass of *hundreds of Jupiters*, configured perfectly. Whether that energy is technically "positive" is a deep and interesting question; whether it's *achievable* is a flat no either way. The sign dispute and the feasibility problem are separate, and only one of them is in play here.

Second — and this is really why I wrote this notebook — *this is what science actually looks like up close.* Not a textbook handing down truths, but credentialed people disagreeing in public about a specific, calculable claim, each pushing the other toward a sharper question. The messiness isn't a failure of the process. It *is* the process. A field that never had unresolved fights like this one wouldn't be doing anything hard.

And it's the test I set for this whole project. The tempting move is to pick the exciting answer (Lentz cracked it!) or the tidy one (the skeptics won). The honest move is to tell you it's open, show you the precise question that would settle it, and resist the pull of either ending. A finding that says "we don't know yet, and here's exactly what we don't know" is worth more than a satisfying story.
