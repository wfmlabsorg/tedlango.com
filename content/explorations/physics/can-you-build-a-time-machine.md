Back in the [wormhole notebook](/explorations/physics/the-wormhole/) I listed four problems and saved the strangest for now. A traversable wormhole isn't only a shortcut through space. Build one, and you've built a shortcut through *time*.

## How a tunnel becomes a time machine

The recipe, worked out by Kip Thorne's group in 1988, is almost too simple. Take a wormhole with two mouths. Leave one at home and load the other onto a fast rocket for a round trip. Special relativity's most famous effect — moving clocks run slow — means the traveling mouth ages less than the stationary one. When they're reunited, the two mouths are permanently *out of sync in time*: one is, say, ten years "ahead" of the other.

Now step through. Enter the mouth that's ten years ahead and you come out the other mouth ten years in the **past**. Step back the other way and you jump ten years into the future. You can loop: go around and arrive before you set out.

![A closed timelike curve: a path through spacetime that loops back to reconnect with its own past — a time machine, allowed by the equations.](/img/physics/time-machine.webp)

Physicists call that closed loop a **closed timelike curve** — a path through spacetime that comes back to its own starting point in time. It's the precise, mathematical version of "time machine." And here's the uncomfortable part: general relativity's equations *allow* such curves. They're not a glitch. They're solutions.

Which immediately raises the question everyone actually cares about: the grandfather paradox. If you can loop back, what stops you from preventing your own trip — and if you prevent it, how did you loop back? Does the universe forbid this? And if it doesn't, how does it avoid the contradiction?

## First, the honest leash

Before the fun part, one anchor. There's a theorem (Frank Tipler, 1976, sharpened by Hawking) that to build a time machine in a *bounded region* — a machine you switch on locally, not one baked into the universe from the start — you need to violate the energy conditions. You need **exotic matter**, the same negative-energy stuff every other notebook in this series has run aground on.

So the time machine is *leashed* to the same problem. You cannot get one more cheaply than you can get a wormhole, and we've seen how hopeless *that* is. Whatever follows is a question about what would happen *if* you'd already solved the impossible part.

## Three camps, no winner

With that said — does physics permit the loop? There are three serious positions, and I genuinely cannot tell you who's right, because the experts can't either.

**Camp 1 — The universe defends itself.** Stephen Hawking's *Chronology Protection Conjecture* (1992). His argument: at the very instant a closed timelike curve is about to form, quantum vacuum fluctuations pile up around the loop and blow up toward infinity, and the resulting energy wrecks the machine before it can switch on. The universe, he wrote, "makes history safe for historians." There's even a rigorous 1997 theorem (Kay, Radzikowski and Wald) showing that the ordinary laws of quantum physics genuinely *break down* at that moment — which supports Hawking, though, crucially, it proves the *theory fails there*, not that the time machine is forbidden.

**Camp 2 — Loops are fine; only consistent stories happen.** Igor Novikov and Thorne's group take the opposite view: maybe the universe allows time machines but only permits *self-consistent* histories. They studied a billiard ball rolled through a wormhole to knock its earlier self off course — the mechanical grandfather paradox — and found something remarkable: there's *always* a self-consistent solution, usually many. The ball that looks destined to create a paradox instead glances off in just the way that lets the whole loop hang together. You can't kill your grandfather — not because a force stops you, but because, in every consistent history, you simply don't. David Deutsch showed a quantum version works too. The price is steep and strange: the future constrains the past, and free will gets complicated.

**Camp 3 — Nobody can know yet.** Matt Visser and others point out that Hawking's blow-up isn't airtight. In 1998, Li and Gott found an explicit case where the vacuum energy stays perfectly finite at the would-be time machine — no infinity, no protection. And the calculations on all sides break down right at the Planck scale, exactly where the answer is decided. Their honest verdict: this question cannot be settled with current physics. It needs a theory of quantum gravity we don't have.

## Where this leaves us

This is, by a wide margin, the most genuinely open question in the whole project. Not "probably impossible" like the warp drive's energy bill, and not "firmly bounded" like the quantum inequalities — actually, sincerely *open*, with three credible camps and no decisive evidence between them.

But notice where it bottoms out. Hawking's protection: unproven below the Planck scale. Li and Gott's counterexample: same. The self-consistency picture: silent on whether such spacetimes can form. Every road runs into the same wall — **quantum gravity**, the missing theory. And that's the quiet thread running under this entire series: each time a door is genuinely open rather than closed, it's open *into the same room* — the one part of physics we don't yet have.
